# Positioning — mytakeoff.ai Messaging Framework

## The Pivot

**FROM:** "Accurate construction estimates powered by AI"
→ Implies production-grade precision. Sets expectations the product can't meet. Invites scrutiny on numbers the validation data doesn't support.

**TO:** "Instant ballpark estimates for residential remodels and ADUs — know which leads are worth chasing."
→ Honest about what it is (ballpark, range-based). Positions value around time saved and decision quality, not numerical precision. Lets accuracy improve behind the scenes without breaking the promise.

## Core Value Proposition

mytakeoff.ai helps residential contractors make faster go/no-go decisions on leads. Upload a floor plan, get a cost range in under 60 seconds, and decide whether a project deserves a full takeoff — before you've driven to the site or spent hours pricing it out.

**It's not replacing your estimating process. It's the filter you run before you start one.**

## Value Props by ICP Segment

### ADU Builders (Strongest Fit)

**Why it fits:** The engine's hardcoded assumptions — slab-on-grade foundation, gable roof at 4/12 pitch, single-story — are literally the standard ADU spec. This isn't a workaround; it's a match.

**Key messages:**
- "Built for the way ADUs are actually built"
- "Slab, gable, single-story — the assumptions match your builds"
- "Ballpark an ADU project in 60 seconds from the floor plan"
- "Know if a client's budget is in the right zip code before you quote"

**Use case:** ADU feasibility conversations with homeowners. "Here's roughly what this will cost at Budget / Standard / Premium levels."

### Small Residential Remodelers (Good Fit for Lead Qualification)

**Why it fits:** Remodelers waste time pricing jobs that were never going to close. This gives them a fast filter.

**Key messages:**
- "Stop spending 3 hours on a takeoff for a client with a $20K budget on a $80K job"
- "Show clients a range before you commit to a site visit"
- "Qualify leads faster — upload the plan, get a ballpark, make the call"

**Use case:** Lead qualification. Client sends a floor plan and asks "how much?" — you upload it, get a range, and decide if it's worth pursuing.

**Caveat in copy:** Position as "starting point," not "the estimate." Remodels involve demolition, existing conditions, and change orders that the engine doesn't model.

### Design-Build Firms (Useful for Early Conversations)

**Why it fits:** Design-build firms need to anchor scope and budget early, before detailed design is done. A fast range helps frame conversations.

**Key messages:**
- "Anchor the budget conversation before design is finalized"
- "Give clients Budget / Standard / Premium ranges to set expectations"
- "Pre-bid scope anchoring — your starting point, not your final number"

**Use case:** Initial client meeting. "Based on this floor plan, here's the range we're looking at. Let's talk about where you want to land."

## Language to Use

- **Ballpark**, **range**, **starting point**, **rough estimate**
- **Budget / Standard / Premium** (the three-tier framing the product actually outputs)
- **Lead qualification**, **go/no-go**, **worth chasing**
- **60 seconds**, **before your site visit**, **before you price it out**
- **Upload a floor plan** (what the user actually does)
- **Low / mid / high ranges** (what the product actually outputs)

## Language to Avoid

- ~~Accurate~~ (unless qualified: "area accuracy" in technical context only)
- ~~Precise~~, ~~exact~~
- ~~AI-powered estimates~~ as the headline (buries the value prop under tech jargon)
- ~~Replace your estimator~~, ~~replace your takeoff process~~
- ~~Bid-ready~~, ~~quote-ready~~, ~~purchase-order ready~~
- ~~Within X% of contractor benchmarks~~ (not defensible)
- ~~Professional-grade estimates~~ (implies bid quality)
- ~~Guaranteed~~ anything

## The Honesty Plays (Trust Builders)

These aren't weaknesses to hide — they're trust signals for a contractor audience that has been burned by overpromising software.

1. **"This is a ballpark, not a bid."** Say it plainly. Contractors respect tools that know what they are.
2. **"Your real takeoff is still your real takeoff."** We're not trying to replace their process. We're trying to save them from wasting it on bad leads.
3. **"Ranges, not point estimates."** The product outputs low/mid/high. Lean into this — it's actually how contractors think about early-stage pricing.
4. **"Best on clean plans, improving on messy ones."** If we reference accuracy at all, be honest about input quality dependency.
5. **"Built for ADUs and single-story residential."** Specificity builds credibility. Saying "we're great at everything" makes contractors trust you less.

## Tone

- **Direct.** Contractors don't read marketing fluff. Short sentences. No buzzwords.
- **Confident but bounded.** "This is what we do well" without implying "this is all you need."
- **Peer-to-peer.** Talk like someone who understands the business, not someone selling to it.
- **No apologies.** Don't frame limitations as shortcomings. Frame them as honest scoping. "We do X. We don't do Y. Here's why X is useful."
